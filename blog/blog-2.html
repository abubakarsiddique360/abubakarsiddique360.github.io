AI and discovery

The Untold AI Race

How DeepMind moved from games to AlphaFold and reshaped science.

When we play chess, we scan the board, anticipate moves, and make calculated decisions before even touching a pawn. Imagine that same process handled entirely by software. This vision marked a breakthrough era for technology, and it began long before the current AI boom.

While the world is only now waking up to generative AI, the real revolution was pioneered by Demis Hassabis and DeepMind. Google recognized the potential early and acquired DeepMind to stay ahead.

Mastering games: the proving grounds

DeepMind's journey started with complex games, each milestone proving that machines could handle uncertainty, creativity, and long-term planning.

AlphaGo shocked the world in 2016 by defeating Lee Sedol, one of the greatest Go players.

AlphaZero taught itself chess, shogi, and Go from scratch, discovering new strategies.

AlphaStar showed that AI could handle real-time strategy under uncertainty.

The scientific revolution: AlphaFold

The true masterpiece came when DeepMind turned to biology. For over 50 years, scientists struggled with the protein-folding problem: predicting 3D protein structure from amino acid sequences.

A single PhD student used to spend years determining one protein structure. AlphaFold now predicts them in seconds.

AlphaFold effectively solved this grand challenge and democratized access to protein structures.

In recognition of this contribution, Demis Hassabis (with John Jumper and David Baker) received the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry.

The game is not the goal. The game teaches us how to solve the hardest problems of science.
