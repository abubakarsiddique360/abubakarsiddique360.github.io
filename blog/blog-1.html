Research notes

A beginner guide to research in computer science

Answers to the most common questions CS students ask before starting research.

1. Conference paper vs research paper vs review paper

A research paper presents original findings, methods, or analyses. It can be published in a conference, a journal, or as a preprint.

A conference paper is a research paper presented at an academic conference. It is often shorter, reviewed faster, and focuses on work-in-progress ideas. It can lead to an extended journal version.

A review paper does not present new experiments. It summarizes, classifies, and analyzes existing research on a specific topic and helps the community understand the state of the art.

Review papers can be published in conferences or journals, but you must check the call for papers.

2. How to find a research gap and decide if you need experiments

Finding a gap

  • Read recent papers and check the future work sections.
  • Compare approaches and spot missing settings like real-time or low-resource use.
  • Ask if you can improve accuracy, speed, scalability, or deployment.
  • Use Connected Papers, Litmaps, or Semantic Scholar to map related work.

Do I need experiments?

  • New algorithms usually need implementation and benchmarking.
  • Theory papers require proofs, but simulations can strengthen the work.
  • Systems and AI research almost always expect experiments.
Rule of thumb: for systems, AI, networking, or security, a practical evaluation is expected.

3. Lab or practical work as a CS student

Most CS research can be done on your laptop or free cloud platforms (Colab, Kaggle, GitHub Codespaces). For large-scale training, use university clusters or student cloud credits.

4. Do I need to write a review paper before a research paper?

Not necessarily. Many researchers start directly with original work. A literature survey helps you understand the field, spot gaps, and can become a publication if done well.

Beginner strategy: write a short survey, then refine it into a formal review if it is publishable.

5. How to use and cite data from others

  • Cite the original dataset or paper and mention how you accessed it.
  • If you reproduce a figure, state it is reproduced or adapted with citation.
  • Check licenses, especially for commercial use.
  • Never copy text without quotes and citation.

6. Where to publish - conference or journal?

In computer science, conferences often have higher visibility and faster publication cycles. Workshops can be a good entry point for beginners.

  • Workshops: smaller, supportive, often co-located with major conferences.
  • Conferences: rigorous review and fast turnaround.
  • Journals: slower but allow more detailed coverage.
Beginner path: short paper to workshop, expand to conference, then extend to journal.

7. Getting help from seniors and teachers

  • Professors can guide direction, review drafts, and connect collaborators.
  • Senior students can help with setup, debugging, and writing style.
  • Show initiative by bringing a concrete idea or short literature review.

8. Inviting researchers from platforms like Google Scholar

You can reach out to potential collaborators respectfully. Read their work, keep the message short, and explain what you can contribute.

Many collaborations start with a polite email. Be clear and patient.

9. A smart strategy for managing citations

  1. Add papers to Zotero or Mendeley as soon as you read them.
  2. Use Connected Papers to save related work early.
  3. Use BibTeX or citation plugins while writing.
  4. Keep a growing bibliography instead of waiting until the end.
